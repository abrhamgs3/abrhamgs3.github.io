Profile

Dr. Abrha Megos Meressa is an economist and research fellow whose work lies at the intersection of environmental economics, behavioral and experimental economics, and agricultural development policy. He is affiliated with the Department of Agricultural Economics at Mekelle University in Tigray, Ethiopia, and conducts collaborative research with the Norwegian University of Life Sciences (NMBU) in Ås, Norway.

His empirical program applies a range of rigorous quantitative methods—generalized method of moments (GMM) estimation, discrete choice experiments, Heckman two-stage selection models, difference-in-differences designs with remote sensing proxies, and mixed logit models—to pressing questions at the intersection of climate adaptation, human decision-making, and smallholder agricultural markets in Sub-Saharan Africa.

Dr. Meressa’s research has been published in Discover Sustainability, Bio-based and Applied Economics, and the Journal of Sustainable Development, with ongoing working papers available via SSRN. His projects span household surveys in Ethiopia’s major agricultural zones, discrete choice experiments with coffee and livestock farmers, and satellite-based welfare measurement in conflict-affected regions.

Research & Publications

My scholarship is organized across three interlocking research pillars. Each applies rigorous quantitative and experimental methods to examine environmental sustainability, human decision-making, and agricultural development in Sub-Saharan Africa—with particular depth in the Ethiopian context.

01

Climate Variability & Environmental Economics

Examining how climate change reshapes agricultural systems in Sub-Saharan Africa, with methodological focus on GMM estimation, logit-based technology adoption models, and behavioral survey instruments to evaluate adaptive capacity, green energy transitions, and conservation preferences.

  1. 2024 Published

    The impacts of climate change on peasant’s crop production in major crop producing zones in Ethiopia

    Discover Sustainability, Springer Nature  ·  2024

    GMM Estimation Panel Survey Data Satellite Precipitation Irrigation Adaptation
    PDF DOI

    This study examines the causal effects of climate variability on smallholder crop yields across major agricultural zones in Ethiopia using a generalized method of moments (GMM) estimator to address the dynamic nature of crop responses to climatic shocks. Drawing on longitudinal household survey data merged with satellite-derived precipitation and temperature records, we find that cumulative rainfall deficits during critical growing windows reduce staple grain yields by 12–18 percent per standard deviation shock. We further document that access to small-scale irrigation infrastructure significantly moderates yield volatility, with irrigated plots exhibiting 40 percent lower yield variance in drought years. Policy simulations suggest that expanding micro-irrigation coverage to the bottom income quintile of smallholder farmers could avert substantial production losses under mid-century climate scenarios projected for Ethiopia's major agricultural zones, underscoring irrigation investment as a priority climate adaptation instrument.

  2. WP Working Paper

    Farmers’ Climate Change Skepticism and Preferences for Forest Conservation

    SSRN Working Paper  ·  Available at Social Science Research Network

    Household Survey Preference Elicitation Information Treatment Design Conservation Policy
    SSRN

    Economic analyses of conservation policy typically assume that rural populations hold accurate priors about climate risks. This paper challenges that assumption by documenting widespread farmer skepticism about anthropogenic climate change in highland Ethiopia and analyzing how this skepticism shapes preferences for community forest conservation programs. Using a representative survey of 800 farming households paired with behavioral preference elicitation tasks, we show that climate skeptics are significantly less willing to participate in community-managed forest conservation schemes, even when economic co-benefits are explicitly communicated. Information treatments emphasizing local, immediate manifestations of climate change—rather than global framing—partially close this participation gap, increasing stated uptake rates by 14 percentage points among the most skeptical respondents. The findings suggest that policy communication design matters substantially for building the farmer coalitions needed to sustain voluntary conservation programs in highland Ethiopia.

  3. 2026 Forthcoming

    Understanding biogas adoption for sustainable energy transition among farm households in Northern Ethiopia

    Discover Sustainability, Springer Nature  ·  2026  ·  Ofla Woreda Survey Study

    Logit Adoption Model Household Survey (N=620) Technology Diffusion Clean Energy Barriers
    PDF DOI

    The uptake of household biogas technology represents a critical pathway for sustainable energy transitions in rural Sub-Saharan Africa, yet adoption rates remain far below potential. This study examines the drivers of and barriers to biogas adoption among farm households in Ofla Woreda, Northern Ethiopia, combining a household survey of 620 respondents with logistic regression and qualitative methods. Awareness of biogas technology is high at 78 percent of respondents, but actual adoption remains below 12 percent. Key barriers include high upfront capital costs, perceived technical complexity, and limited access to credit and extension support. Household assets, cattle ownership, and proximity to demonstration sites are the strongest positive predictors of adoption. The findings point to the need for targeted subsidy programs, community demonstration facilities, and integrated extension support to accelerate diffusion of clean cooking technologies in rural Northern Ethiopia as part of a broader green energy transition strategy.

02

Behavioral & Experimental Economics

Employing discrete choice experiments, mixed logit and random parameter models, willingness-to-pay elicitation, and framed field experiments to understand how farmers’ preferences, beliefs, and behavioral heterogeneity shape adoption of climate-resilient agricultural varieties and sustainable land management strategies.

  1. 2024 Published

    Not my cup of coffee: Farmers’ preferences for coffee variety traits – Lessons for crop breeding in the age of climate change

    Bio-based and Applied Economics  ·  Firenze University Press

    Discrete Choice Experiment Mixed Logit Model Willingness-to-Pay Climate-Resilient Traits
    PDF DOI

    This study employs a discrete choice experiment (DCE) to elicit smallholder farmers' preferences for traits of improved coffee varieties in Ethiopia, with particular attention to climate-resilient genetic attributes including drought tolerance, heat resistance, and pest resistance. A sample of 480 coffee farmers across three agro-ecological zones participated in choice tasks that varied variety attributes including yield potential, disease resistance, maturation period, and climate resilience traits. Results from a mixed logit model reveal that farmers place the highest implicit willingness to pay on disease resistance—particularly coffee wilt resistance—followed by yield level and drought tolerance. Younger farmers and those who have experienced productivity losses from recent drought episodes show systematically higher valuation of climate-resilient traits. The findings provide concrete attribute-specific preference data to guide trait prioritization in national coffee breeding programs operating under accelerating climate change, with direct implications for Ethiopia's coffee export competitiveness.

  2. WP Working Paper

    Farmers’ Behavior and Provision of Improved Ecosystem Services: A Choice Experiment of Ethiopian Farmers’ Adoption of Sustainable Management Strategies

    SSRN Working Paper  ·  Available at Social Science Research Network

    Choice Experiment Random Parameter Logit Latent Class Analysis Ecosystem Services
    SSRN

    We investigate Ethiopian smallholder farmers' willingness to adopt sustainable land management practices that provide improved ecosystem services using a framed choice experiment. Farmers were presented with combinations of sustainable management strategies—including soil and water conservation, agroforestry integration, and reduced chemical inputs—along with associated labor requirements, subsidy levels, and expected yield changes. A random parameter logit model identifies substantial unobserved preference heterogeneity for ecosystem service programs, with latent class analysis revealing three distinct farmer types: subsidy-oriented adopters, co-benefit-motivated adopters, and resistant non-adopters. The persistent non-adopter class, comprising approximately 28 percent of the sample, indicates that payment for ecosystem services programs alone are insufficient to achieve full participation, and must be complemented by extension services addressing technical knowledge gaps and targeted social norm interventions for resistant farming households.

03

Market Participation, Commercialization & Welfare Analysis

Applying Heckman two-stage selection models, satellite-based remote sensing welfare proxies, and difference-in-differences identification strategies to study how transaction costs, cooperative institutions, and conflict exposure shape smallholder market integration and household welfare outcomes across Ethiopian contexts.

  1. 2026 Forthcoming

    Market Participation and Sales Intensity among Dairy Farmers in Ethiopia: Evidence from Sululta District

    2026  ·  Sululta District, Oromia, Ethiopia

    Heckman Two-Stage Selection Transaction Cost Theory Cooperative Impact Extension Services
    PDF

    This paper analyzes the determinants of market participation and commercial intensity among smallholder dairy farmers in Sululta District, a major periurban milk-shed supplying Addis Ababa, Ethiopia. Using a Heckman two-stage selection model to jointly estimate participation and volume decisions, we find that transaction costs—proxied by distance to collection points, road quality, and market information access—are the dominant constraint on participation, particularly for female-headed households. Membership in dairy cooperatives increases the probability of market participation by 22 percentage points and raises milk sales volumes by 35 percent among participants, demonstrating that cooperative institutions substantially reduce effective transaction costs. Extension service contacts have a positive but smaller effect on participation intensity. Results identify infrastructure investment, cooperative strengthening, and targeted extension as three complementary pillars of a commercialization strategy for Ethiopia's rapidly expanding periurban dairy sector.

  2. 2023 Published

    Measuring Multidimensional Poverty in Conflict Zones: A Remote Sensing Method for the Tigray Conflict

    Journal of Sustainable Development  ·  Canadian Center of Science and Education

    Difference-in-Differences Remote Sensing / Satellite Nighttime Light Intensity Conflict Economics
    PDF DOI

    Standard household survey instruments are rarely deployable in active conflict zones, creating severe data gaps precisely when welfare monitoring is most needed. This paper develops and validates a remote sensing methodology for proxying multidimensional poverty dynamics in conflict-affected areas, using the Tigray Conflict (2020–2022) as a test case. We construct a composite welfare index from high-resolution satellite data including nighttime light intensity, vegetation indices, and settlement structure measures, validated against pre-conflict survey benchmarks. Applying a difference-in-differences estimator that exploits the geographic spread of the conflict over time, we find that conflict exposure reduced our remote-sensing welfare proxy by an average of 34 percent in directly affected woredas, with significantly larger losses in zones experiencing sustained fighting. The methodology provides a replicable template for real-time welfare monitoring in survey-inaccessible conflict settings across Sub-Saharan Africa and other fragile-state contexts.

Instruction & Curriculum

Teaching

I teach across microeconomic theory, environmental and resource economics, agricultural economics, and applied econometrics—connecting theoretical frameworks to empirical methods and real-world agricultural and development challenges, with instructional delivery at both Mekelle University and NMBU.

Undergraduate Core Courses

  • Principles of Microeconomics

    Consumer theory, production economics, market equilibrium, welfare analysis, game theory fundamentals, and market failures including public goods and externalities. Emphasis on graphical and algebraic tools with policy applications to agricultural and environmental markets.

  • Environmental & Resource Economics

    Economic frameworks for analyzing environmental externalities, pollution control instruments (Pigouvian taxes, emission standards, tradeable permits), renewable and non-renewable resource extraction theory, and non-market valuation methods including contingent valuation and discrete choice experiments.

  • Agricultural Economics

    Smallholder farm production decisions, input and output market structure, agricultural supply chains, price stabilization policy, cooperative economics, and the institutional economics of agricultural development with focus on Sub-Saharan African contexts.

  • Introduction to Econometrics

    OLS estimation, statistical inference, model specification testing, heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, serial correlation, binary outcome models, and introductory panel data methods. Hands-on instruction using STATA and R with applied datasets from Ethiopian agricultural surveys.

Graduate Advanced Courses

  • Advanced Environmental Economics

    Dynamic optimization for renewable and non-renewable resource management, welfare economics under uncertainty, climate economics and social cost of carbon, mechanism design for environmental policy, emissions trading scheme design, and frontier empirical topics in environmental economics.

  • Applied Econometrics & Causal Inference

    Instrumental variables, generalized method of moments, difference-in-differences with staggered rollout, regression discontinuity design, synthetic control, and randomization inference. Emphasis on research design, pre-analysis plan writing, and replication of published studies. Extensive STATA and R applications.

  • Behavioral & Experimental Methods in Development

    Design and analysis of economic experiments in development contexts: preference elicitation using multiple price lists and BDM mechanisms, discrete choice experiment design, conjoint analysis, lab-in-the-field implementation, Heckman selection models for survey non-response, and structural estimation of discrete choice behavioral models.
