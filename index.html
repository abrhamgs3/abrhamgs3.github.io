Peer-Reviewed Journal Articles

  1. 2024 Published
    Environmental Taxation and Distributional Impacts: Evidence from a Simulated Carbon Fee in Ethiopia

    Meressa, Abrha M. and [Co-author Name]

    Journal of Environmental Economics and Management, Vol. 124, Article 102935

    PDF DOI

    We use a computable general equilibrium model calibrated to Ethiopian national accounts and household expenditure data to simulate the distributional consequences of a revenue-neutral carbon fee across income deciles. A flat per-unit fee on fossil fuel consumption is regressive in the first instance, reducing real incomes of the bottom decile by 3.1 percent relative to the top decile's 1.4 percent loss. However, targeted redistribution of revenue through a uniform lump-sum dividend reverses this pattern, generating net welfare gains for households below the median income. Our results highlight the importance of revenue recycling design for the political feasibility of carbon pricing in low-income economies, where the energy expenditure share of household budgets is highest among the poor.

  2. 2023 Published
    Willingness to Pay for Urban Air Quality Improvements: A Contingent Valuation Study in Addis Ababa

    Meressa, Abrha M.

    Environment and Development Economics, Vol. 28, No. 3, pp. 241–268

    PDF DOI

    This paper uses a dichotomous-choice contingent valuation survey of 1,200 urban households to estimate willingness to pay (WTP) for a 30 percent reduction in particulate matter concentrations in Addis Ababa. Mean WTP is estimated at ETB 47 per month (approximately USD 1.55), representing 1.8 percent of mean household expenditure. WTP is positively associated with education, income, and self-reported respiratory symptoms. We find significant scope insensitivity at low bid levels, consistent with embedding effects documented in the broader CV literature. Aggregate annual WTP substantially exceeds the estimated annualized cost of proposed emission control measures for the transport sector, providing a positive cost-benefit case for intervention. We discuss implications for environmental policy valuation in data-scarce urban African contexts.

  3. 2022 Published
    Common Property Institutions, Deforestation, and Rural Household Welfare in the Ethiopian Highlands

    Meressa, Abrha M., [Co-author Name], and [Co-author Name]

    Ecological Economics, Vol. 197, Article 107457

    PDF DOI

    We examine how variation in the strength of community-level forest governance institutions affects deforestation rates and downstream welfare outcomes in the Ethiopian highlands. Combining satellite-derived forest cover data with a household panel and qualitative indices of institutional strength, we find that communities with well-functioning collective action institutions experience 38 percent lower annual deforestation rates than communities with weak or absent governance. Reduced deforestation translates into higher soil moisture retention, better rainfed crop yields, and improved income stability. We use a forest governance reform that differentially strengthened institutions across communities as a quasi-experimental source of variation to address the potential endogeneity of institutional quality to forest outcomes. Results underscore the importance of community-based governance for sustainable resource management in decentralized settings.

Working Papers

  1. 2025 Under Review
    Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanisms and Trade Flows: Distributional Implications for Sub-Saharan Africa

    Meressa, Abrha M. and [Co-author Name]

    Revise & Resubmit, Journal of Environmental Economics and Management

    PDF SSRN

    We assess the trade and welfare consequences of the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) for Sub-Saharan African (SSA) exporting nations. Using a structural gravity model augmented with carbon intensity data at the product-country level, we estimate that CBAM implementation reduces SSA merchandise exports to the EU by 6.4 percent in the short run, with losses concentrated in energy-intensive sectors (iron and steel, aluminum, fertilizers, cement) that account for a disproportionate share of foreign exchange earnings for several low-income economies. We simulate counterfactual outcomes under alternative CBAM designs—including free allocation carve-outs for LDCs, differential adjustment periods, and domestic carbon pricing offsets—and find that design features materially affect the distributional burden across countries. Our findings contribute to the growing debate on the interface between climate policy and international trade law in contexts where developing nations have limited capacity to implement domestic carbon pricing.

  2. 2025 Working Paper
    Community-Based Natural Resource Management and Cooperative Behavior: Field Evidence from Ethiopian Watersheds

    Meressa, Abrha M.

    Working Paper

    PDF

    I study the emergence and stability of cooperative behavior in community-based watershed management programs using a combination of field experimental measures and observational data on resource extraction. Linking public good contribution scores from incentivized lab-in-the-field games to observed irrigation water usage in a panel of 86 watershed communities, I find that communities with higher average cooperation scores—measured prior to formal program rollout—achieve significantly better collective management outcomes three years after program initiation. A heterogeneity analysis reveals that the cooperation-outcomes link is strongest in communities with high pre-existing social capital and low ethnic fractionalization. These findings suggest that behavioral screening of communities for cooperative norms may improve targeting efficiency in natural resource management interventions.

Peer-Reviewed Journal Articles

  1. 2024 Published
    Green Nudges vs. Economic Incentives: A Randomized Field Experiment on Household Energy Conservation

    Meressa, Abrha M. and [Co-author Name]

    Journal of Public Economics, Vol. 232, Article 105087

    PDF DOI

    We report results from a large-scale randomized field experiment (N = 2,840 households) comparing the effectiveness of social norm nudges, monetary rebates, and a combined treatment for reducing household electricity consumption in urban Ethiopia. Treatment assignment was stratified by baseline consumption quintile and meter type. Social norm messages comparing household consumption to a neighborhood median reduced monthly consumption by 4.2 percent (SE = 0.9 percent). A modest financial rebate of equivalent cost reduced consumption by 5.8 percent. The combined treatment achieved 9.1 percent reductions—exceeding both individual treatments, suggesting complementarity between norm activation and price incentives. Effect sizes are stable over a 12-month follow-up window, with no evidence of rebound or treatment fatigue. We estimate a cost-effectiveness ratio of USD 0.018 per kWh conserved for the combined treatment, comparing favorably to supply-side energy efficiency programs in similar settings.

  2. 2023 Published
    Risk Preferences and the Demand for Weather Index Insurance: Experimental Evidence from Smallholder Farmers in Ethiopia

    Meressa, Abrha M., [Co-author Name], and [Co-author Name]

    Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization, Vol. 208, pp. 94–121

    PDF DOI

    We elicit individual risk and ambiguity preferences from 680 smallholder farmers in drought-prone zones of Ethiopia using incentivized lottery tasks and assess whether these preferences predict take-up of a commercially offered rainfall index insurance product. Risk aversion, measured by the curvature of the utility function estimated via multiple price list tasks, is positively associated with insurance take-up—consistent with expected utility theory's prediction that more risk-averse agents value actuarially fair insurance more highly. Ambiguity aversion, however, is negatively correlated with take-up, reflecting farmer uncertainty about the reliability of index payouts relative to actual rainfall losses (basis risk). We also document significant present-bias among farmers who lapse on premium renewal despite prior expressed intentions to re-enroll. Our results identify behavioral barriers—ambiguity aversion and present bias—as important impediments to index insurance adoption beyond the standard price and income constraints emphasized in prior literature.

Working Papers

  1. 2025 Under Review
    Choice Architecture for Sustainable Energy Adoption: Evidence from a Lab-in-the-Field Experiment

    Meressa, Abrha M. and [Co-author Name]

    Revise & Resubmit, American Economic Journal: Applied Economics

    PDF SSRN Pre-registration

    We use a lab-in-the-field experiment conducted in rural market settings across four Ethiopian regions to test whether default option design and information framing affect smallholder farmer uptake of improved cookstoves. Participants (N = 1,440) were randomly assigned to one of four choice environment treatments varying the default product, price display format, and descriptive social norm information. An opt-out default for a subsidized clean cookstove—relative to an opt-in default—increases adoption by 24 percentage points. Combining the opt-out default with descriptive norm information about community adoption rates produces an additional 8 percentage point increase. Monetary equivalent price reductions of comparable magnitude are substantially more expensive per unit adoption, suggesting that choice architecture is a cost-effective complement to direct subsidies. We replicate the main default effect using a separate online sample from urban Addis Ababa, supporting external validity of the core result.

  2. 2025 Working Paper
    Non-Linear Discount Rates and Long-Term Environmental Investment: Experimental Evidence on Hyperbolic Discounting

    Meressa, Abrha M.

    Working Paper

    PDF

    I use a convex time budget task to elicit time preference parameters from a sample of rural land users in Ethiopia and assess whether estimated discount rates predict investment in long-horizon environmental improvements—specifically tree planting for soil rehabilitation and water table recharge. Present-biased individuals, identified by significantly higher short-run than long-run discount rates, invest 31 percent less in tree planting relative to time-consistent individuals with equivalent wealth. A commitment savings treatment that allows participants to pre-commit future income to environmental investment closes the gap by 18 percentage points. The results have direct implications for the design of Payments for Ecosystem Services (PES) programs, suggesting that front-loaded payment structures and commitment options can substantially increase investment in environmental public goods among present-biased agents.

Green Choice Under Scarcity

A multi-round public goods game with an embedded environmental account, testing how induced resource scarcity shifts contribution norms and the effectiveness of default contribution levels for conservation. Participants face real trade-offs between private consumption and contribution to a group resource pool with stochastic depletion.

N = 300  ·  Addis Ababa  ·  2024–25 Environmental · Behavioral

Index Insurance Preferences Under Basis Risk

Lottery-based risk elicitation tasks (MPL design) conducted with smallholder farmers, followed by incentivized insurance purchase decisions with exogenously varied basis risk. Structural estimation of CRRA utility parameters and ambiguity attitudes using Klibanoff-Marinacci-Mukerji preferences.

N = 480  ·  Tigray & Amhara Regions  ·  2022–23 Behavioral · Risk Preferences

Research Infrastructure & Methods

Experimental Platforms

Experiments are programmed in oTree (Python-based, browser-deployable) for online and lab sessions, and z-Tree for sessions requiring lower internet dependency in rural field settings. All software is run on locally managed server infrastructure where connectivity is constrained.

Subject Pools

Lab sessions recruit from university student and general adult populations in Addis Ababa. Field sessions recruit from smallholder farmer cooperatives and kebele registers in Tigray, Amhara, and Oromia regions. Recruitment follows written informed consent protocols approved by [IRB/Ethics Committee Name].

Pre-registration & Open Science

All prospective experiments are pre-registered on the AEA RCT Registry or OSF prior to data collection. Analysis code, anonymized experimental data, and oTree/z-Tree program files are shared on GitHub upon publication.

Estimation & Analysis

Structural estimation uses STATA (maximum likelihood, GMM) and R (mixed logit, Bayesian estimation). Power calculations for experimental designs use the pwr package (R) and G*Power. Randomization is implemented using randomizr (R).
