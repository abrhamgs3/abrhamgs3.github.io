Dr. Abrha Megos Meressa

PhD in Economics

I am an economist whose research sits at the intersection of development economics, agricultural markets, and institutional analysis, with a primary focus on Sub-Saharan Africa. My work examines how credit market imperfections, land tenure institutions, and productivity shocks shape household welfare and long-run poverty dynamics. I combine structural econometric models with quasi-experimental designs and detailed panel survey data to generate findings that speak directly to development policy.

Research & Publications

My research investigates the structural determinants of poverty, welfare, and long-run development in low-income economies, with particular emphasis on East and Sub-Saharan Africa.

Peer-Reviewed Journal Articles

  1. 2024 Published

    Agricultural Productivity Shocks and Rural Poverty Traps: Evidence from the Ethiopian Highlands

    Meressa, Abrha M., [Co-author Name], and [Co-author Name]

    Journal of Development Economics, Vol. 168, Article 103268

    PDF DOI

    This paper examines the causal effect of rainfall-induced agricultural productivity shocks on long-run welfare outcomes in the Ethiopian highlands. Using a panel of 4,500 rural households over fifteen years and exploiting plausibly exogenous variation in seasonal rainfall, we identify a persistent poverty trap mechanism operating through livestock asset depletion. Households below a critical asset threshold are unable to smooth consumption in the face of negative shocks, leading to durable welfare losses that persist well beyond the shock period. We estimate that a one standard deviation negative productivity shock reduces the probability of escaping poverty by 12 percentage points for asset-poor households, compared to a statistically insignificant effect for wealthier households. Our findings highlight the need for targeted safety nets and complementary asset-building interventions in drought-prone rural environments.

  2. 2023 Published

    Financial Inclusion and Consumption Smoothing Among Smallholder Farmers: Microeconomic Evidence from Eastern Africa

    Meressa, Abrha M. and [Co-author Name]

    World Development, Vol. 162, 106130

    PDF DOI

    We study the impact of mobile money adoption on household consumption smoothing among smallholder farmers in Eastern Africa. Exploiting the staggered geographic rollout of mobile money services as a quasi-experimental source of variation, we find that access to mobile financial services increases per capita consumption by 8.4 percent and reduces the sensitivity of consumption to income shocks by approximately 30 percent. Effects are strongest for female-headed households and those in remote areas historically excluded from the formal financial system. The mechanism operates primarily through improved informal risk-sharing networks and reduced reliance on costly emergency credit. These results underscore the potential of fintech interventions to address structural credit market failures in low-income agricultural settings.

  3. 2021 Published

    Returns to Education and Labor Market Outcomes in Urban Ethiopia: A Gender-Disaggregated Analysis

    Meressa, Abrha M.

    Journal of African Economies, Vol. 30, No. 4, pp. 312–341

    PDF DOI

    This paper estimates private returns to schooling in urban Ethiopian labor markets using instrumental variable methods that address the endogeneity of educational attainment. Exploiting distance to the nearest secondary school as an instrument for years of completed schooling, I find that an additional year of education increases monthly earnings by 9.2 percent on average. Disaggregating by gender reveals substantially higher returns for women (12.8 percent) relative to men (6.9 percent), consistent with strong positive selection of women into formal wage employment in an environment where female labor force participation remains constrained. Returns are non-linear, with the largest earnings gains accruing at the transition to secondary and tertiary levels. The findings carry implications for education financing policy and for targeted interventions designed to close gender gaps in formal sector employment in rapidly urbanizing Sub-Saharan African economies.

Working Papers

  1. 2025 Under Review

    Credit Market Frictions, Technology Adoption, and Agricultural Transformation in Sub-Saharan Africa

    Meressa, Abrha M. and [Co-author Name]

    Revise & Resubmit, American Journal of Agricultural Economics

    PDF SSRN

    We examine how credit market imperfections constrain adoption of yield-enhancing agricultural technologies among smallholder farmers in Sub-Saharan Africa. Using a novel household panel dataset matched with satellite-derived soil quality measures and granular rainfall data, we document that credit-constrained households adopt improved seed varieties and chemical fertilizers at rates 28–35 percent lower than unconstrained households with otherwise equivalent land quality. We develop and estimate a dynamic discrete choice model of technology adoption that incorporates liquidity constraints, and identify structural parameters via a combination of observed adoption patterns and a small-scale randomized experiment that exogenously relaxes borrowing constraints for a subset of households. Model counterfactual simulations indicate that eliminating credit market frictions would increase fertilizer adoption by 40 percent and raise average maize yields by 18 percent, translating into substantial aggregate welfare gains.

  2. 2025 Working Paper

    Land Tenure Security, Investment Incentives, and Long-Run Productivity: Quasi-Experimental Evidence from Ethiopia's Land Registration Program

    Meressa, Abrha M.

    Working Paper

    PDF

    This paper exploits the phased rollout of Ethiopia's land registration and certification program to estimate the causal effect of formalized tenure security on smallholder farm investment and long-run agricultural productivity. Using a difference-in-differences design with a rich household panel spanning the pre- and post-reform period, I find that land certification increases investment in soil conservation measures by 22 percent and in tree planting by 34 percent. These investment responses translate into measured productivity gains of approximately 9 percent for grain crops over a five-year horizon. Heterogeneity analysis reveals that effects are concentrated among female-headed households and smaller landholders, the populations for whom informal tenure was historically most precarious. The results provide some of the cleanest evidence to date on the investment channel linking formal property rights to agricultural productivity in Sub-Saharan Africa.
